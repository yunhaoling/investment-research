    Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) Analysis

    A valuation method that estimates the intrinsic value of a company based on
    its expected future cash flows, discounted back to present value.

    Core Formula

    Present Value = FCF / (1 + r)^t

    • FCF = Free Cash Flow in period t
    • r = Discount rate (usually WACC)
    • t = Time period

    Key Components

    Component Description
    Free Cash Flow (FCF) Cash generated after capital expenditures
    WACC Weighted Average Cost of Capital — the discount rate
    Terminal Value Value of all cash flows beyond the forecast period
    Intrinsic Value Sum of all discounted FCFs + Terminal Value

    Steps to Build a DCF

    1. Project Free Cash Flows (typically 5–10 years)
    2. Calculate WACC (cost of equity + cost of debt, weighted)
    3. Estimate Terminal Value (Gordon Growth Model or Exit Multiple)
    4. Discount everything back to present value
    5. Compare intrinsic value to current market price

    Terminal Value Methods

    Method Formula Best For
    Gordon Growth Model FCF × (1+g) / (WACC - g) Stable, mature companies
    Exit Multiple EBITDA × Industry Multiple Cyclical or high-growth companies

    Key Takeaways

    • If intrinsic value > market price → stock may be undervalued
    • If intrinsic value < market price → stock may be overvalued
    • Small changes in WACC or growth rate have outsized impact on output
    • Always apply a margin of safety (e.g., buy at 20–30% below intrinsic value)

    Limitations

    • Highly sensitive to assumptions (WACC, growth rate)
    • Terminal value often accounts for 60–80% of total value
    • Difficult to apply to unprofitable or early-stage companies

    Best Used With

    • DuPont Analysis (understand ROE quality before projecting FCF)
    • Porter's Five Forces (validate long-term growth assumptions)
    • SWOT Analysis (stress-test key risks to cash flow projections)
